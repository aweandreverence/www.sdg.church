Biographies of Faithful Saints

“Therefore, since we have so great a cloud of witnesses surrounding us, let us also lay aside every encumbrance and the sin which so easily entangles us, and let us run with endurance the race that is set before us.”

Throughout history, countless faithful Christians have glorified God through their extraordinary works. From composers who inscribed Soli Deo Gloria on their manuscripts to scientists who saw the heavens declaring God's glory, from missionaries who gave their lives for the gospel to reformers who stood for truth — these are their stories.

Composers & Musicians

Johann Sebastian Bach

1685-1750

Composer

German composer and musician of the Baroque period, widely regarded as one of the greatest composers of all time. His works include the Brandenburg Concertos, the Mass in B minor, and hundreds of cantatas.

Faith

A devout Lutheran who inscribed 'Soli Deo Gloria' (To God Alone Be the Glory) on his manuscripts. He composed extensively for the church and viewed his musical gifts as given by God for His glory.

Sources

  • Bach: Music in the Castle of Heaven — By John Eliot Gardiner. Explores Bach's faith and its influence on his compositions
  • Bach and the Soli Deo Gloria Tradition — Documentaries exploring Bach's practice of inscribing SDG on his manuscripts

George Frideric Handel

1685-1759

Composer

German-born British Baroque composer famous for his operas, oratorios, and concertos. Best known for 'Messiah,' one of the most performed choral works in Western music.

Faith

A committed Christian whose masterwork 'Messiah' sets to music the life of Jesus Christ from prophecy through resurrection. Upon completing the 'Hallelujah Chorus,' he reportedly said, 'I did think I did see all Heaven before me, and the great God Himself.'

Sources
  • George Frideric Handel — Wikipedia — Biography including composition of Messiah
  • Messiah (Handel) — Wikipedia — Detailed history of the oratorio and its biblical text
  • Handel's Messiah: Comfort for God's People — By Calvin Stapert. Explores the theological depth of Handel's masterwork

Felix Mendelssohn

1809-1847

Composer

German composer, pianist, and conductor of the early Romantic period. Known for his symphonies, concertos, and oratorios including 'Elijah.'

Faith

Raised in a Jewish family that converted to Christianity, Mendelssohn was a devout Lutheran. His oratorio 'Elijah' and symphony 'Reformation' reflect his deep Christian faith. He also revived interest in Bach's sacred works.

Sources
  • Felix Mendelssohn — Wikipedia — Biography including his Christian faith and sacred compositions
  • Elijah (Mendelssohn) — Wikipedia — His major biblical oratorio

Fanny Crosby

1820-1915

Hymn Writer

American mission worker, poet, lyricist, and composer who wrote over 8,000 hymns and gospel songs. Blind from infancy, she became one of the most prolific hymnists in history.

Faith

Despite losing her sight as an infant, Crosby devoted her life to glorifying God through music. She wrote beloved hymns including 'Blessed Assurance,' 'To God Be the Glory,' and 'All the Way My Savior Leads Me.'

Sources
  • Fanny Crosby — Wikipedia — Biography of the prolific blind hymn writer
  • Fanny Crosby: An Autobiography — Crosby's own account of her life and faith journey
  • The Story of Fanny Crosby — Documentaries about her extraordinary life and 8,000+ hymns

Authors & Poets

C.S. Lewis

1898-1963

Author & Apologist

British writer and lay theologian, author of 'The Chronicles of Narnia,' 'Mere Christianity,' and 'The Screwtape Letters.' Fellow of Magdalen College, Oxford and later Professor at Cambridge.

Faith

A former atheist who converted to Christianity in 1931, Lewis became one of the most influential Christian apologists of the 20th century. He described his conversion in 'Surprised by Joy' and defended the faith in works like 'Mere Christianity.'

Sources
  • C. S. Lewis — Wikipedia — Comprehensive biography including his conversion from atheism
  • Surprised by Joy — By C.S. Lewis. His autobiographical account of his conversion to Christianity
  • Mere Christianity — By C.S. Lewis. One of the most influential Christian apologetics books of the 20th century

J.R.R. Tolkien

1892-1973

Author & Philologist

English writer, philologist, and academic, best known for 'The Hobbit' and 'The Lord of the Rings.' Professor of Anglo-Saxon at Oxford University.

Faith

A devout Roman Catholic whose faith deeply influenced his literary works. He was instrumental in C.S. Lewis's conversion to Christianity. He described 'The Lord of the Rings' as 'a fundamentally religious and Catholic work.'

Sources
  • J. R. R. Tolkien — Wikipedia — Biography including his Catholic faith and its influence on his works

  • Tolkien's faith and Middle-earth — Wikipedia article on Christian themes in Tolkien's works

John Milton

1608-1674

Poet

English poet and intellectual who served as a civil servant during the Commonwealth. Best known for the epic poem 'Paradise Lost,' considered one of the greatest works in the English language.

Faith

A deeply devout Puritan Christian, Milton's magnum opus 'Paradise Lost' aimed to 'justify the ways of God to men.' His works are saturated with biblical themes and theological reflection.

Sources
  • John Milton — Wikipedia — Biography including his Puritan faith and theological writings
  • Paradise Lost — Wikipedia — His epic Christian poem retelling the Fall of Man

Fyodor Dostoevsky

1821-1881

Novelist

Russian novelist, philosopher, and journalist. His major works include 'Crime and Punishment,' 'The Brothers Karamazov,' and 'The Idiot.'

Faith

After a period of doubt, Dostoevsky experienced a profound renewal of faith during his imprisonment in Siberia. His novels explore the depths of human sin and the transforming power of Christ's love. He wrote, 'If anyone proved to me that Christ was outside the truth... I would remain with Christ.'

Sources

G.K. Chesterton

1874-1936

Author & Apologist

English writer, philosopher, and Christian apologist. Author of the Father Brown mysteries, 'Orthodoxy,' 'The Everlasting Man,' and over 80 books.

Faith

Initially an agnostic, Chesterton converted first to Anglicanism and then to Roman Catholicism. His book 'Orthodoxy' describes his intellectual journey to faith. C.S. Lewis credited 'The Everlasting Man' as key to his own conversion.

Sources
  • G. K. Chesterton — Wikipedia — Comprehensive biography including his conversion journey
  • Orthodoxy — By G.K. Chesterton. His autobiographical account of arriving at Christian faith through reason
  • The Everlasting Man — By G.K. Chesterton. The book C.S. Lewis credited with helping him toward Christianity

John Bunyan

1628-1688

Author & Preacher

English writer and Puritan preacher best known for 'The Pilgrim's Progress,' one of the most significant works of English literature and the most widely read Christian allegory ever written.

Faith

A Baptist preacher who was imprisoned for 12 years for preaching without a license. During his imprisonment he wrote 'The Pilgrim's Progress,' an allegory of the Christian journey from sin to salvation that has never been out of print.

Augustine of Hippo

354-430

Theologian & Author

Early Christian theologian and philosopher from North Africa. Bishop of Hippo Regius and author of 'Confessions' and 'The City of God,' among the most influential works in Western Christianity.

Faith

After years of dissolute living and exploring various philosophies, Augustine dramatically converted to Christianity, famously hearing a child's voice saying 'Take up and read.' His 'Confessions' is one of the first autobiographies in Western literature and a testament to God's grace.

Sources
  • Augustine of Hippo — Wikipedia — Comprehensive article on the most influential Church Father
  • Confessions — By Augustine. His autobiographical account of conversion, one of the most important works in Christian literature

Leo Tolstoy

1828-1910

Novelist

Russian writer regarded as one of the greatest authors of all time. Best known for 'War and Peace' and 'Anna Karenina.'

Faith

After a spiritual crisis in midlife, Tolstoy underwent a profound Christian conversion. He devoted the latter part of his life to understanding and following the teachings of Jesus, writing 'A Confession' and 'The Kingdom of God Is Within You.'

Sources

George MacDonald

1824-1905

Author & Minister

Scottish author, poet, and Christian minister. A pioneering figure in fantasy literature, writing works such as 'Phantastes,' 'The Princess and the Goblin,' and 'Lilith.'

Faith

A Congregationalist minister whose theology of God's love permeated all his writings. C.S. Lewis called MacDonald his 'master' and credited MacDonald's 'Phantastes' with 'baptizing his imagination.'

Sources
  • George MacDonald — Wikipedia — Biography of the minister and fantasy pioneer who influenced C.S. Lewis

Scientists

Isaac Newton

1643-1727

Physicist & Mathematician

English mathematician, physicist, and astronomer who formulated the laws of motion and universal gravitation, developed calculus, and made groundbreaking contributions to optics.

Faith

Newton wrote more about theology than science. He believed the elegant design of the solar system pointed to God and wrote, 'This most beautiful system of the sun, planets and comets, could only proceed from the counsel and dominion of an intelligent and powerful Being.'

Sources
  • Isaac Newton — Wikipedia — Biography including his extensive theological writings
  • Religious views of Isaac Newton — Wikipedia — Detailed article on Newton's Christian beliefs and theological works
  • Principia Mathematica — By Isaac Newton. Contains the General Scholium where he writes about God's dominion over the cosmos

Blaise Pascal

1623-1662

Mathematician, Physicist & Philosopher

French mathematician, physicist, inventor, and philosopher. Made foundational contributions to probability theory, projective geometry, and the development of the mechanical calculator.

Faith

After a mystical experience on November 23, 1654 (his 'Night of Fire'), Pascal devoted his life to defending Christianity. His 'Pensées' remains one of the most eloquent defenses of the Christian faith ever written. He is the author of 'Pascal's Wager.'

Sources
  • Blaise Pascal — Wikipedia — Biography including his 'Night of Fire' conversion experience
  • Pensées — Wikipedia — His famous unfinished defense of the Christian faith
  • Pascal's Wager — Wikipedia — His famous philosophical argument for belief in God

Michael Faraday

1791-1867

Physicist & Chemist

English scientist who contributed to the study of electromagnetism and electrochemistry. His discoveries include electromagnetic induction, diamagnetism, and electrolysis.

Faith

A devout member of the Sandemanian church (a small Christian sect), Faraday's faith was central to his life. He served as a deacon and elder and saw no conflict between science and his Christian beliefs, viewing the study of nature as the study of God's creation.

Sources
  • Michael Faraday — Wikipedia — Biography including his Sandemanian faith
  • Glasite — Wikipedia — About the Sandemanian/Glasite church Faraday belonged to

Gregor Mendel

1822-1884

Geneticist & Friar

Augustinian friar and scientist who discovered the fundamental laws of inheritance through his work with pea plants. Known as the 'Father of Genetics.'

Faith

An Augustinian friar who served as abbot of his monastery, Mendel's scientific work was conducted within the context of his religious life. He saw no contradiction between his faith and his scientific research.

Sources
  • Gregor Mendel — Wikipedia — Biography of the Augustinian friar who founded genetics

George Washington Carver

1864-1943

Agricultural Scientist & Inventor

American agricultural scientist and inventor who developed hundreds of products using peanuts, sweet potatoes, and soybeans. Born into slavery, he became one of the most prominent Black scientists of his era.

Faith

A devout Christian who attributed all his discoveries to God. He famously said, 'I love to think of nature as an unlimited broadcasting station, through which God speaks to us every hour, if we will only tune in.' He taught a weekly Bible class for over 30 years.

Sources

Louis Pasteur

1822-1895

Chemist & Microbiologist

French chemist and microbiologist renowned for his discoveries of the principles of vaccination, microbial fermentation, and pasteurization. He is known as the 'Father of Microbiology.'

Faith

A devout Catholic who maintained his faith throughout his scientific career. He stated, 'The more I study nature, the more I stand amazed at the work of the Creator.'

Johannes Kepler

1571-1630

Astronomer & Mathematician

German astronomer, mathematician, and astrologer who discovered the three laws of planetary motion, foundational to Isaac Newton's theory of universal gravitation.

Faith

A devout Lutheran who saw his scientific work as a form of worship. He wrote, 'I was merely thinking God's thoughts after Him. Since we astronomers are priests of the highest God in regard to the book of nature, it benefits us to be thoughtful.'

Sources
  • Johannes Kepler — Wikipedia — Biography including his Lutheran faith and view of science as worship

Robert Boyle

1627-1691

Chemist & Physicist

Anglo-Irish natural philosopher, chemist, physicist, and inventor, regarded as the first modern chemist. Best known for Boyle's Law relating gas pressure and volume.

Faith

A deeply devout Christian who wrote extensively on theology and funded Bible translations. He established the Boyle Lectures for defending Christianity against atheism and viewed science as a way to understand God's creation.

Sources
  • Robert Boyle — Wikipedia — Biography including his extensive theological writings and Bible translations
  • Boyle Lectures — Wikipedia — The lecture series Boyle established to defend Christianity

James Clerk Maxwell

1831-1879

Physicist & Mathematician

Scottish physicist responsible for the classical theory of electromagnetic radiation, unifying electricity, magnetism, and light. Einstein called Maxwell's work 'the most profound and the most fruitful that physics has experienced since the time of Newton.'

Faith

A deeply committed evangelical Presbyterian who prayed regularly and saw his physics as revealing God's glory. He wrote, 'I think that the results which each man arrives at in his attempts to harmonize his science with his Christianity ought not to be regarded as having any significance except to the man himself.'

Francis Collins

1950-present

Geneticist

American physician-geneticist who led the Human Genome Project and served as director of the National Institutes of Health (NIH) from 2009 to 2021.

Faith

A former atheist who converted to Christianity after reading C.S. Lewis's 'Mere Christianity.' He wrote 'The Language of God: A Scientist Presents Evidence for Belief' and founded BioLogos to explore the harmony between science and faith.

Sources

Mathematicians

Leonhard Euler

1707-1783

Mathematician & Physicist

Swiss mathematician, physicist, and astronomer who made foundational contributions to numerous areas of mathematics. He is considered one of the greatest mathematicians of all time.

Faith

A devout Calvinist who wrote a defense of Christianity called 'Letters to a German Princess,' which included arguments for the existence of God. He held daily family prayers and composed hymns.

Sources
  • Leonhard Euler — Wikipedia — Biography including his Calvinist faith and defense of Christianity
  • Letters to a German Princess — Wikipedia — His work containing arguments for the existence of God

Bernhard Riemann

1826-1866

Mathematician

German mathematician who made lasting contributions to analysis, number theory, and differential geometry, including the Riemann hypothesis and Riemannian geometry (which provided the mathematical foundation for Einstein's general relativity).

Faith

The son of a Lutheran pastor, Riemann was a devout Christian throughout his life. He originally planned to study theology before pursuing mathematics.

Georg Cantor

1845-1918

Mathematician

German mathematician who created set theory and established the importance of one-to-one correspondence between sets, defining infinite and well-ordered sets.

Faith

A devout Lutheran who believed his theory of transfinite numbers was communicated to him by God. He saw the absolute infinite as an expression of the nature of God.

Sources
  • Georg Cantor — Wikipedia — Biography including his belief that God communicated set theory to him

Artists & Architects

1475-1564

Artist, Sculptor & Architect

Italian sculptor, painter, architect, and poet of the High Renaissance. Created the ceiling of the Sistine Chapel, the statue of David, and the Pietà.

Faith

A devout Catholic whose greatest works depicted biblical scenes and Christian themes. His Sistine Chapel ceiling telling the story of Genesis and his Pietà depicting Mary holding the body of Christ are among the most recognizable Christian artworks ever created.

1606-1669

Painter & Printmaker

Dutch Golden Age painter and printmaker, generally considered one of the greatest visual artists in history. Known for biblical paintings, self-portraits, and masterful use of light and shadow.

Faith

A deeply devout Protestant Christian, Rembrandt painted over 300 biblical scenes, more than any other major Western artist. His 'The Return of the Prodigal Son' is considered one of the most powerful visual depictions of God's grace.

Albrecht Dürer

Artist & Printmaker

German painter, printmaker, and theorist of the German Renaissance. Known for his woodcuts, engravings, and paintings including 'Praying Hands.'

Faith

A devout Christian who embraced the Protestant Reformation. His famous 'Praying Hands' drawing and numerous biblical engravings reflect his deep faith. He admired Martin Luther and incorporated Reformation theology into his later works.

Architect

Spanish architect and the best-known practitioner of Catalan Modernism. His masterwork, the Sagrada Família basilica in Barcelona, remains under construction more than a century after his death.

Faith

A devout Catholic whose later years were entirely dedicated to building the Sagrada Família as an offering to God. He lived ascetically and attended Mass daily. He is currently a candidate for canonization in the Catholic Church.

Reformers & Theologians

Martin Luther

1483-1546

Reformer & Theologian

German professor of theology, priest, and seminal figure in the Protestant Reformation. His Ninety-five Theses challenged the Catholic Church's practices and launched the Reformation.

Faith

Luther's rediscovery of justification by faith alone (sola fide) through Scripture alone (sola scriptura) transformed Christianity. His translation of the Bible into German made Scripture accessible to ordinary people.

Sources
  • Martin Luther — Wikipedia — Comprehensive biography of the father of the Reformation
  • Ninety-five Theses — Wikipedia — The document that launched the Protestant Reformation

John Calvin

1509-1564

Reformer & Theologian

French theologian, pastor, and reformer who was a principal figure in the development of the system of Christian theology later called Calvinism. His 'Institutes of the Christian Religion' is a foundational work of Protestant theology.

Faith

Calvin's theology emphasized the sovereignty of God, the authority of Scripture, and salvation by grace alone through faith alone. His work in Geneva established a model for Reformed churches worldwide.

Sources
  • John Calvin — Wikipedia — Comprehensive biography of the Reformed theologian
  • Institutes of the Christian Religion — Wikipedia — His foundational work of Protestant theology

Jonathan Edwards

1703-1758

Theologian & Preacher

American revivalist preacher, philosopher, and Congregationalist Protestant theologian. Key figure in the First Great Awakening and third president of Princeton University.

Faith

Edwards is widely regarded as America's greatest theologian. His sermon 'Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God' is one of the most famous sermons in American history. His theological works on the nature of true virtue and religious affections remain deeply influential.

Sources

Charles Haddon Spurgeon

1834-1892

Preacher & Author

English Particular Baptist preacher known as the 'Prince of Preachers.' Pastor of the Metropolitan Tabernacle in London, he frequently preached to audiences of more than 10,000 people.

Faith

Spurgeon was converted at age 15 and began preaching immediately. He published thousands of sermons and authored numerous books. He founded a pastors' college, orphanages, and other charitable organizations, all flowing from his deep love for Christ.

Sources
  • Charles Spurgeon — Wikipedia — Biography of the 'Prince of Preachers'

John Wesley

1703-1791

Theologian & Evangelist

English cleric, theologian, and evangelist who co-founded the Methodist movement. His open-air preaching brought the gospel to millions across England.

Faith

After his famous 'Aldersgate experience' in 1738 where his heart was 'strangely warmed,' Wesley dedicated his life to evangelism. He traveled 250,000 miles on horseback, preached over 40,000 sermons, and sparked a revival that transformed England.

Sources
  • John Wesley — Wikipedia — Comprehensive biography of the founder of Methodism
  • Aldersgate experience — Wikipedia — Wesley's famous heart-warming conversion experience

George Whitefield

1714-1770

Evangelist & Preacher

Anglican cleric and evangelist who was one of the founders of Methodism and the evangelical movement. He preached to enormous crowds on both sides of the Atlantic and was a key figure in the Great Awakening.

Faith

Whitefield was one of the most powerful preachers in Christian history, known for his dramatic delivery and deep conviction. Benjamin Franklin estimated he could be heard by 30,000 people at once. He preached over 18,000 sermons in his lifetime.

Sources
  • George Whitefield — Wikipedia — Biography of the Great Awakening evangelist

William Wilberforce

1759-1833

Politician & Reformer

British politician and philanthropist who led the movement to abolish the slave trade in the British Empire. Member of Parliament for 45 years.

Faith

After his evangelical conversion in 1785, Wilberforce devoted his life to two 'great objects': the abolition of the slave trade and the reformation of manners (moral reform). His Christian faith was the driving force behind his tireless campaign against slavery.

Sources

Dietrich Bonhoeffer

1906-1945

Theologian & Martyr

German Lutheran pastor, theologian, and anti-Nazi dissident. Author of 'The Cost of Discipleship' and 'Life Together.' Executed by the Nazis at Flossenbürg concentration camp.

Faith

Bonhoeffer's theology of 'costly grace' challenged cheap Christianity. He helped found the Confessing Church in opposition to Nazi influence and participated in a plot to assassinate Hitler. His final words were, 'This is the end — for me, the beginning of life.'

Pastors & Missionaries

Billy Graham

1918-2018

Evangelist

American evangelist and ordained Southern Baptist minister whose broadcasts and world tours spanning over six decades made him one of the most influential Christian leaders of the 20th century. He preached to live audiences of nearly 215 million people in more than 185 countries.

Faith

Known as 'America's Pastor,' Graham preached a simple message of salvation through Jesus Christ. He served as spiritual advisor to multiple U.S. Presidents and founded the Billy Graham Evangelistic Association. His integrity and humility set a standard for public Christian ministry.

Sources
  • Billy Graham — Wikipedia — Comprehensive biography of the most prominent evangelist of the 20th century

Jim Elliot

1927-1956

Missionary & Martyr

American Christian missionary who was killed along with four companions during Operation Auca, an attempt to evangelize the Huaorani people of Ecuador. He was 28 years old. His death galvanized a generation of missionaries.

Faith

Elliot famously wrote in his journal, 'He is no fool who gives what he cannot keep to gain what he cannot lose.' His wife Elisabeth Elliot later returned to minister to the very tribe that killed him, and many Huaorani eventually became Christians.

Sources
  • Jim Elliot — Wikipedia — Biography of the missionary martyr in Ecuador
  • Operation Auca — Wikipedia — The failed missionary expedition that cost five lives
  • Through Gates of Splendor — By Elisabeth Elliot. Account of the five missionaries' martyrdom by Jim's widow

Hudson Taylor

1832-1905

Missionary to China

British Protestant Christian missionary to China and founder of the China Inland Mission (now OMF International). He spent 51 years in China and is regarded as one of the most important missionaries of all time.

Faith

Taylor pioneered a new approach to missions by adopting Chinese dress and customs, living by faith without soliciting funds, and opening inland China to the gospel. The CIM he founded became the largest mission agency in the world, eventually bringing over 800 missionaries to China.

Sources
  • Hudson Taylor — Wikipedia — Biography of the pioneering missionary to inland China

Eric Liddell

1902-1945

Olympic Runner & Missionary

Scottish Olympic gold medalist (400m, 1924 Paris Olympics) who refused to run his best event (100m) because the heat was on a Sunday. He later served as a missionary in China, where he died in a Japanese internment camp during World War II.

Faith

Liddell famously said, 'God made me fast. And when I run, I feel His pleasure.' After his Olympic triumph, he devoted his life to missionary work in China. His story was immortalized in the Academy Award-winning film 'Chariots of Fire.'

Sources

William Carey

1761-1834

Missionary to India

English Baptist minister, missionary, and translator known as the 'Father of Modern Missions.' He founded the Serampore College and Serampore University (the first degree-granting university in India) and translated the Bible into numerous Indian languages.

Faith

Carey's famous motto was 'Expect great things from God; attempt great things for God.' He labored for decades in India, translating the Bible into Bengali, Sanskrit, and many other languages, establishing schools, and fighting social evils like sati (widow burning).

Sources
  • William Carey (missionary) — Wikipedia — Biography of the 'Father of Modern Missions'
  • An Enquiry into the Obligations of Christians to Use Means for the Conversion of the Heathens — By William Carey. The pamphlet that launched the modern missions movement

Adoniram Judson

1788-1850

Missionary to Burma

American Congregationalist and later Baptist missionary who served in Burma (Myanmar) for nearly 40 years. He was the first American foreign missionary sent overseas and translated the entire Bible into Burmese.

Faith

Judson endured imprisonment, torture, the deaths of two wives and several children, and decades of hardship for the sake of the gospel. He completed the Burmese Bible translation and established churches that endure to this day.

Sources
  • Adoniram Judson — Wikipedia — Biography of the first American foreign missionary
  • To the Golden Shore: The Life of Adoniram Judson — By Courtney Anderson. Definitive biography of Judson

David Livingstone

1813-1873

Missionary & Explorer

Scottish Congregationalist pioneer Christian missionary, doctor, and explorer in Africa. He was the first European to see Victoria Falls and made extensive explorations of central Africa while seeking to end the slave trade.

Faith

Livingstone believed that Christianity and commerce together could end the African slave trade. He spent over 30 years in Africa, often alone, driven by his faith and desire to bring the gospel to unreached peoples. His body was buried in Westminster Abbey.

Sources
  • David Livingstone — Wikipedia — Biography of the missionary-explorer of Africa
  • Missionary Travels and Researches in South Africa — By David Livingstone. His own account of his African journeys

George Müller

1805-1898

Evangelist & Orphanage Director

Prussian-born evangelist and director of the Ashley Down orphanage in Bristol, England. He cared for over 10,000 orphans during his lifetime and established 117 schools offering Christian education to over 120,000 children.

Faith
